Corporation tax is one of those things every limited company director knows they have to deal with, but a surprising number of business owners couldn't confidently explain how it actually works. That's not a criticism — tax legislation isn't exactly bedtime reading — but a basic understanding of corporation tax can genuinely change how you plan your finances, time your spending, and structure your business.

This guide breaks down corporation tax in plain English: who pays it, how it's calculated, when it's due, and what you can legitimately do to reduce it.

## What Is Corporation Tax?

Corporation tax is the tax UK limited companies pay on their profits — not their revenue, but what's left after allowable expenses, salaries, and other deductible costs have been taken out. It applies to trading profits, investment profits, and chargeable gains from selling assets such as property or shares.

Unlike income tax, there's no automatic notification telling you what you owe. Corporation tax is a self-assessed system — your company is responsible for calculating its own liability, reporting it accurately to HMRC, and paying on time, even though HMRC isn't going to chase you with a bill in advance.

## Who Needs to Pay It?

Any UK limited company, including most clubs, co-operatives, and other unincorporated associations, is liable for corporation tax on its profits. This applies whether you're a small one-person consultancy or a larger company with dozens of employees.

Sole traders and ordinary partnerships don't pay corporation tax — they pay income tax on business profits through Self Assessment instead. This is one of the key differences founders need to understand when deciding whether to incorporate.

## How Is Corporation Tax Calculated?

![Calculating corporation tax step by step](https://iris.b-cdn.net/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/how-to-calculate-corporation-tax-iris.jpg)

The basic calculation works like this:

1. Start with your company's total income for the accounting period
2. Deduct allowable business expenses (staff costs, rent, equipment, professional fees, and so on)
3. Deduct any capital allowances on qualifying equipment or assets
4. Apply any reliefs you're entitled to, such as R&D tax relief
5. The result is your taxable profit, which corporation tax is calculated on

The UK currently operates a tiered corporation tax system, with a lower rate applying to profits below a set threshold, a higher main rate applying above an upper threshold, and marginal relief smoothing the transition for profits that fall in between. Where your company sits within these bands has a direct impact on your effective tax rate, which is why profit timing and allowable deductions matter so much.

## Key Corporation Tax Deadlines

Corporation tax deadlines are tied to your company's accounting period, not a fixed calendar date, which trips up a lot of first-time directors. The two key dates to know are:

- **Payment deadline**: usually 9 months and 1 day after the end of your accounting period
- **Filing deadline**: 12 months after the end of your accounting period, for submitting your Company Tax Return (CT600)

It's worth noting the payment deadline comes before the filing deadline — many new directors assume they're the same date and end up paying late as a result. Missing either deadline triggers automatic penalties and interest, regardless of how small the company is.

## Allowable Expenses That Reduce Your Bill

Corporation tax is only charged on profit, so legitimately reducing your taxable profit through proper expense claims is one of the simplest ways to manage your bill. Commonly claimed expenses include:

- Salaries, employer National Insurance, and pension contributions for staff and directors
- Office rent, utilities, and business insurance
- Travel and subsistence for business purposes
- Marketing, professional fees, and software subscriptions
- Equipment and machinery, often through capital allowances

Keeping clean, well-organised records throughout the year — rather than trying to piece things together at year-end — makes it far easier to capture everything you're entitled to deduct.

## Reliefs Worth Knowing About

Beyond standard expense claims, there are several reliefs that can meaningfully reduce a company's corporation tax bill:

- **Annual Investment Allowance** — allows the full cost of qualifying equipment purchases to be deducted from profits in the year of purchase, up to the annual limit
- **R&D tax relief** — available to companies carrying out qualifying research and development, even outside traditional "lab science" settings such as software and manufacturing
- **Loss relief** — trading losses can often be carried forward against future profits or carried back against prior profits, reducing tax once the company returns to profitability
- **Patent Box relief** — a reduced rate of corporation tax on profits earned from patented inventions, for companies that qualify

## Common Mistakes Business Owners Make

![Common corporation tax mistakes to avoid](https://iris.b-cdn.net/wp-content/uploads/2022/08/how-to-calculate-corporation-tax-iris.jpg)

- **Confusing the payment and filing deadlines**, leading to late payment penalties even when the return itself is filed on time
- **Under-claiming legitimate expenses** out of caution, leaving money on the table unnecessarily
- **Not planning around accounting period changes**, which can shift deadlines unexpectedly
- **Leaving tax planning until year-end**, missing opportunities to time purchases or pension contributions effectively
- **Assuming a small company doesn't need to think about corporation tax until it's "big enough"** — even modest profits are taxable from day one

## Why Proactive Planning Beats Reactive Filing

The companies that manage corporation tax best treat it as an ongoing consideration throughout the year, not a once-a-year scramble before the deadline. That means reviewing profit levels regularly, timing larger purchases to make the most of capital allowances, and checking in on reliefs like R&D before the accounting period closes rather than after.
