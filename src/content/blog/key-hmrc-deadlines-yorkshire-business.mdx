Missing an HMRC deadline rarely happens because a business owner doesn't care about compliance. It happens because tax deadlines are scattered across different taxes, different calculations, and different timeframes — and when you're running a business day to day, it's easy for one date to slip through while you're focused on everything else.

For Yorkshire businesses, getting on top of the HMRC calendar isn't just about avoiding penalties, though that matters. Late filing and payment penalties have actually become tougher recently: from 1 April 2026, late Corporation Tax return penalties doubled from £100 to £200, rising to £400 if a return is more than three months overdue. HMRC has also been clear that closing the so-called "tax gap" is a continued priority, with increased filing penalties and expanded compliance powers across multiple taxes this year. Staying ahead of deadlines has genuinely become more important than it was a few years ago.

This guide brings together the key HMRC deadlines that matter most for Yorkshire SMEs, sole traders, and limited company directors, organised by tax type.

## Self Assessment Deadlines

Self Assessment affects sole traders, partners, company directors with additional income, and anyone with untaxed income above HMRC's reporting thresholds.

- **5 October** — deadline to register for Self Assessment if you're newly self-employed or have new untaxed income
- **31 October** — deadline for paper tax returns (increasingly rare, but still a legal option)
- **31 January** — deadline for online tax returns and the balancing payment for the previous tax year, plus the first payment on account for the current year
- **31 July** — deadline for the second payment on account

Missing the 31 January deadline triggers an automatic £100 penalty, even if you owe no tax, with further penalties accruing the longer the return remains outstanding, plus daily penalties after three months and additional charges after six and twelve months for particularly late returns.

### Making Tax Digital for Income Tax

This is the deadline change Yorkshire sole traders and landlords most need to be aware of right now. Making Tax Digital for Income Tax Self Assessment is mandatory for self-employed individuals and landlords with gross income from those sources above £50,000, with quarterly digital updates required throughout the year rather than a single annual return. This is a fundamental shift in how often you need to engage with your bookkeeping, and it's catching out businesses that haven't yet moved to compatible cloud software.

## Corporation Tax Deadlines

Corporation Tax deadlines are tied to your company's accounting period, not a fixed calendar date, which is the single biggest source of confusion for new directors.

- **Payment deadline**: 9 months and 1 day after the end of your accounting period
- **Filing deadline (CT600)**: 12 months after the end of your accounting period

Note that payment is due *before* filing — a detail that trips up a lot of first-time company directors who assume the two deadlines align. As mentioned above, late filing penalties doubled from April 2026, making it more important than ever to file accurately and on time rather than treating the 12-month mark as a soft deadline.

## VAT Deadlines

VAT-registered businesses typically file returns quarterly, although some operate under annual or monthly schemes depending on which VAT accounting scheme they use.

- **Standard quarterly returns**: due one calendar month and 7 days after the end of your VAT quarter, with payment due by the same date
- **Annual Accounting Scheme**: one return a year, with regular advance payments throughout the year
- **Making Tax Digital for VAT**: all VAT-registered businesses must keep digital records and file through MTD-compatible software — this isn't optional and has been a legal requirement for several years now

Because VAT deadlines repeat every quarter, missing one isn't a one-off mistake — it tends to be a sign that the underlying record-keeping process needs attention.

## Payroll and PAYE Deadlines

For Yorkshire businesses with employees, payroll compliance runs on its own clock, separate from annual tax deadlines.

- **Real Time Information (RTI)**: pay and deductions must be reported to HMRC on or before each payday, not after
- **PAYE payment deadline**: the 22nd of the month following the deduction (or the 19th if paying by post)
- **P60s**: must be issued to employees by 31 May following the end of the tax year
- **P11Ds** (for taxable benefits and expenses): due by 6 July following the end of the tax year, with Class 1A National Insurance due by 22 July

Auto-enrolment pension duties also run continuously throughout the year, not just at a single annual deadline — employers must assess new staff, manage opt-ins and opt-outs correctly, and complete a re-declaration of compliance with The Pensions Regulator periodically.

## Companies House Deadlines (Often Confused With HMRC)

While Companies House isn't HMRC, its deadlines run alongside tax obligations and are frequently mixed up by business owners:

- **Annual accounts**: due 9 months after your company's financial year end (for private companies)
- **Confirmation statement**: due at least once every 12 months
- **Changes to company details**: generally must be reported within 14 days

Missing Companies House deadlines carries its own separate penalty regime, escalating the later accounts are filed, and persistent failures can ultimately lead to a company being struck off the register.

## What Happens If You Miss a Deadline

The exact penalty depends on the tax involved, but the general pattern across HMRC's penalty regimes is the same: an initial fixed penalty, followed by escalating daily or percentage-based penalties the longer the issue goes unresolved, plus interest charged on any tax paid late. With increased late filing penalties now in effect for Corporation Tax and HMRC's broader push to close the tax gap, the cost of letting deadlines slip has gone up across the board in 2026.

Beyond the direct financial cost, repeated late filing can also affect how HMRC views your business more broadly, sometimes triggering closer scrutiny of future returns.

## A Practical Way to Stay on Top of Deadlines

The businesses that consistently hit their deadlines tend to share a few habits:

- They use cloud accounting software that flags upcoming deadlines automatically, rather than relying on memory
- They build in a buffer of at least a week before any payment deadline, in case of bank processing delays or last-minute queries
- They review their accounting period and VAT quarter dates at the start of each year, rather than assuming they're the same as the calendar year
- They have a single point of contact — whether in-house or an accountant — who's responsible for tracking every deadline across every tax, rather than leaving it split across different people
