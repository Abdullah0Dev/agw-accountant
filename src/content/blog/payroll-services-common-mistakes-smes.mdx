Payroll is one of those business functions that nobody notices when it's working — and everybody notices the moment it isn't. Get an employee's pay wrong, miss a pension deadline, or file late with HMRC, and the fallout ranges from awkward conversations to genuine penalties and damaged trust within the team.

For SMEs without a dedicated in-house payroll specialist, mistakes are more common than most business owners realise — usually not from negligence, but simply because payroll has more moving parts than it first appears. Here are the most common payroll mistakes UK SMEs make, and how to avoid them.

## 1. Missing Real Time Information (RTI) Deadlines

Under HMRC's Real Time Information system, employers are required to report pay and deductions to HMRC on or before each payday, not after. A surprising number of SMEs still treat this as something that can be done "a few days later" — but late RTI submissions can trigger penalties, and persistent lateness affects HMRC's view of your business's overall compliance record.

**The fix:** Build payroll processing into your calendar with enough buffer before payday to catch errors, rather than running it on the day itself.

## 2. Misclassifying Workers

![Worker classification mistakes in payroll](https://media.licdn.com/dms/image/v2/C5112AQGWvX2psmP-cA/article-cover_image-shrink_600_2000/article-cover_image-shrink_600_2000/0/1520239867728?e=2147483647&v=beta&t=_ixncm6NeHmiYmU-6Fdz4CAKEJrA4zdzPCZKrVAAc4M) 
Whether someone is an employee, a worker, or genuinely self-employed has significant payroll, tax, and employment rights implications — and getting it wrong is one of the costliest mistakes an SME can make. Treating someone as self-employed when HMRC would view them as an employee can lead to backdated PAYE and National Insurance liabilities, sometimes years after the fact.

**The fix:** Review working arrangements properly against HMRC's employment status guidance, particularly for regular contractors or freelancers who work primarily for your business.

## 3. Getting Auto-Enrolment Pension Duties Wrong

Workplace pension auto-enrolment comes with a long list of employer duties — assessing eligible staff, enrolling them within strict timeframes, calculating contributions correctly, and re-enrolling staff who've opted out every three years. SMEs frequently fall down on:

- Missing the enrolment deadline for new eligible employees
- Calculating contributions on the wrong earnings basis
- Failing to complete the re-declaration of compliance to The Pensions Regulator
- Not handling opt-outs and opt-ins correctly

The Pensions Regulator actively monitors compliance and issues fines for missed duties, even for businesses with just a handful of staff.

**The fix:** Use payroll software with built-in auto-enrolment functionality, and don't assume "set it up once and forget it" — ongoing duties continue for as long as you employ staff.

## 4. Incorrect Tax Code Application

Using an outdated or incorrect tax code is a common, quietly costly error — it leads to employees being overtaxed or undertaxed, both of which create headaches. Overtaxing damages trust and triggers refund requests; undertaxing can mean an unexpected tax bill for the employee later, which they'll often (understandably) bring straight back to you.

**The fix:** Make sure new starter information (P45 or starter checklist) is processed correctly, and act promptly on tax code notices from HMRC rather than letting them sit.

## 5. Errors With Statutory Payments

Statutory Sick Pay, Statutory Maternity/Paternity Pay, and other statutory entitlements have specific eligibility rules and calculation methods that catch SMEs out regularly — particularly around qualifying periods and average weekly earnings calculations.

**The fix:** Keep accurate records of employee earnings history, since statutory payment eligibility is often based on earnings over a specific reference period, and get these calculations checked rather than estimated.

## 6. Poor Record-Keeping

![Payroll record keeping mistakes](https://www.bookkeeppros.com/blog/wp-content/uploads/2023/08/Top-10-Mistakes-In-Payroll-Processing-850x567.jpg)

HMRC requires payroll records to be kept for a minimum period, covering pay, deductions, leave, and statutory payments. SMEs relying on scattered spreadsheets or informal systems often struggle to produce accurate records when needed — whether for an HMRC enquiry, an employee dispute, or simply year-end reporting.

**The fix:** Use proper payroll software that maintains a clear audit trail automatically, rather than relying on manual records that are easy to lose or misplace.

## 7. Miscalculating Holiday Pay

Holiday pay calculations have become more complex, particularly for employees with irregular hours or variable pay. Using a flat assumption rather than calculating holiday pay based on average earnings over the correct reference period is a common source of underpayment — and a common source of employee disputes.

**The fix:** Make sure your payroll system or provider is applying current holiday pay calculation rules correctly, especially for part-time, casual, or variable-hours staff.

## 8. Not Updating Payroll for Legislative Changes

National Minimum Wage rates, National Insurance thresholds, and statutory payment rates change regularly, often at the start of the tax year. SMEs running payroll manually or through outdated software sometimes miss these updates, leading to underpayment that needs correcting retrospectively — which is far more disruptive than getting it right the first time.

**The fix:** Make sure whoever runs your payroll — whether in-house or outsourced — has a clear process for reviewing legislative updates each tax year.
